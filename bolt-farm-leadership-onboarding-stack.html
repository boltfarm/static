Weekly Rhythm & Operational Excellence

BOLT FARM operates on a disciplined weekly rhythm that ensures clarity, accountability, and continuous improvement.

CEO/Leadership Weekly Cycle

Monday: Priority Setting

Objective: Align the entire organization around top 3 company priorities and cascade to departments.

  • CEO identifies and communicates Top 3 company-wide priorities
  • Each department leader sets their own Top 3 priorities aligned to company goals
  • Priorities published and visible to entire team
  • All subsequent work flows from these priorities

Tuesday–Thursday: Execution & Coaching

Objective: Execute against priorities and provide coaching to leaders.

  • Teams execute 2-week sprints on department priorities
  • Department leaders manage their teams with accountability
  • Mid-week 1:1s with each leader (30–45 min coaching sessions, not problem-solving)
  • Focus: Are you on track? What support do you need? How are you developing your team?

Friday: Scorecard Review

Objective: Review metrics and discuss adjustments for next week.

  • Numbers only—no drama, no excuses
  • KPIs reviewed against targets
  • Wins celebrated, gaps acknowledged
  • Quick adjustments planned for next week
  • No more than 30 minutes per department

Department Sprint System

2-Week Sprints

Every department operates on 2-week sprints with clearly defined Top 3 priorities, KPIs, and deliverables. Department leaders own the direction and enforce accountability within their teams.

What Each Leader Owns:

  • Top 3 Priorities: Clear, measurable goals for the sprint
  • KPIs: Metrics that measure success (daily/weekly)
  • Deliverables: Specific outputs that prove progress
  • Accountability: Team members know exactly what they own and by when

How Leaders Set Their Weekly Rhythm

As a new leader, your rhythm mirrors the CEO's approach:

  1. Monday: You set your Top 3 department priorities aligned to company goals. Communicate to your team.
  2. Tuesday–Thursday: You coach your team members through execution. You don't solve problems—you ask questions and develop judgment.

Decision-Making Frameworks

Every decision at BOLT FARM is made using these frameworks. They ensure speed, clarity, and alignment with our brand and mission.

Elon's 5-Step Algorithm: The Foundation

This algorithm is used for ALL decisions at BOLT FARM—from operations to strategy. It's a filter for excellence.

  1. Question Every Requirement

    Is this truly needed? Challenge the premise. Don't accept "that's how we've always done it."

  2. Delete Any Part or Process You Can

    Remove redundancy, complexity, and waste. Simplicity is a feature, not a compromise.

  3. Simplify and Optimize

    Make what remains more efficient, elegant, and elegant. Can it be done better with fewer resources?

  4. Accelerate Cycle Time

    Can this move faster? Speed compounds advantage. Faster feedback loops lead to better decisions.

  5. Automate as Much as Possible

    Use AI and systems over human effort. People are for judgment and connection, not repetition.

Apply This to Everything

Deciding on a new guest check-in process? Use Elon's algorithm. Designing a new team workflow? Use Elon's algorithm. This discipline makes us faster and better than competitors.

The 6 Decision Filters

Before making any decision, ask yourself: Does this pass the 6 filters?

1. Guest Experience
Does this enhance the guest experience? Every decision must improve how guests feel, what they remember, and whether they return.
30% Weight
2. Brand Alignment
Does it align with BOLT FARM's luxury and wellness values? Does it strengthen or dilute our brand promise?
25% Weight
3. Scalability
Can this be replicated across 50+ locations? If we can't scale it, we're building a liability, not an asset.
20% Weight
4. Profitability
Will this increase revenue or reduce costs? No decision is made purely on emotion or brand—unit economics matter.
15% Weight
5. Simplicity
Can this be executed without unnecessary complexity? Complex operations fail. Simple operations scale.
10% Weight
6. Sustainability
Does this support eco-friendly practices? We serve nature lovers—our operations must reflect that values alignment.
Holistic

How to Use the Filters

Score each filter as Yes (1) or No (0). If you get "Yes" to 4+ filters (especially the high-weight ones), proceed. If you get "No" to 3+, refine or rework the idea.

Filter Score Decision
5–6 Yes Proceed immediately
3–4 Yes Refine and resubmit
1–2 Yes Rework or reject

Example: Applying the Filters

Scenario: Should we add a spa service at BOLT FARM?
  • ✓ Guest Experience: Yes (wellness is our brand)
  • ✓ Brand Alignment: Yes (luxury + wellness)
  • ? Scalability: Maybe (harder to scale than accommodations)
  • ✓ Profitability: Yes (high-margin service)
  • ✓ Simplicity: No (requires training, licensing, staffing)
  • ✓ Sustainability: Yes (if we use eco-friendly products)

Result: 4–5 Yes out of 6 → Refine the spa model to improve scalability and simplicity, then proceed with a pilot.

Escalation Rules & Authority Framework

Leaders make decisions. Only escalate when necessary. Use the 70% rule to develop judgment and empower your team.

The 70% Rule: Own It

If someone can do it at 70% of your quality, they own it.

Don't escalate. Don't micromanage. Let them learn, fail safely, and build judgment. This is how leaders develop.

When to Escalate (Only 5 Scenarios)

Escalate ONLY when one of these applies:

1. Brand Integrity at Risk ▼

The decision affects BOLT FARM's brand promise, values, or reputation in a material way.

Example: A team member wants to offer 50% discounts to friends. This breaks our pricing integrity and dilutes our luxury position. Escalate.

2. Unbudgeted Spend >$5K ▼

Any expense that exceeds $5,000 and wasn't planned requires approval from the next level of leadership.

Example: You want to hire a contractor for $8K to fix a building issue. Escalate for approval and budget adjustment.

3. Guest Safety or Legal Issue ▼

Any decision involving guest safety, liability, legal compliance, or contracts must be escalated immediately.

Example: A guest reports a safety concern. Don't make a unilateral decision—escalate to leadership and legal immediately.

4. Cross-Department Coordination Required ▼

A decision affects another department and requires alignment or trade-offs to resolve.

Example: You want to change check-in time, which affects guest communications, housekeeping, and the concierge. Too many dependencies—escalate.

5. New Precedent Being Set ▼

This decision creates a new policy or precedent that other teams will follow.

Example: You want to allow team members to work remotely 3 days a week. This is a new precedent that affects culture and operations—escalate for alignment.

How to Escalate: The Framework

When you escalate, you don't bring a problem—you bring clarity. Present three things:

1. The Problem (Clear & Concise)

State what's happening, why it matters, and what the consequence is if unaddressed. Be specific. Avoid drama.

2. 2–3 Options You've Considered

Show you've thought critically. Present options with pros/cons. Demonstrate you're not just passing the buck.

3. Your Recommended Action & Why

Take a stand. Use the 6 decision filters to explain why your recommendation is the best path forward.

Example: Escalation Done Right

Good Escalation

Problem: "Our housekeeping team is unable to turn rooms in the 2-hour window we promised guests because we're understaffed. If we miss this window, it directly impacts guest experience and our Net Promoter Score."

Options:

  • Hire 2 more housekeepers (cost: ~$3K/month, solves problem immediately)
  • Implement a room-cleaning automation tool to reduce manual work (upfront cost: $5K, reduces labor by 20% long-term)
  • Adjust our booking window to 2.5 hours (slightly impacts guest experience, reduces labor pressure)

Recommendation: "I recommend Option 2. It scales with our growth, improves efficiency per Elon's algorithm, and after 18 months, saves us money vs. hiring more staff. It also fits our 'AI-first' operating model. Can we green-light this?"

Bad Escalation

"Our housekeeping is struggling. We need to do something. Should we hire more people?" ← This is lazy escalation. You're not thinking. Don't do this.
